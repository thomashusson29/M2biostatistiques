# Oral preparation on the article

## SLIDE 1

- The article presents a clinical trial on pramipexole, used as an adjunct to an antidepressant, in patients with treatment-resistant unipolar depression.
- The title of the study clearly announces the setting: acute phase, treatment-resistant unipolar depression, randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind trial, conducted in the UK.
- The research question is simple: in adults already treated with an antidepressant but insufficiently improved, does the addition of pramipexole reduce symptoms more than a placebo?
- The primary efficacy endpoint is evaluated at 12 weeks.
- Patients are then followed up to 48 weeks to describe clinical evolution, tolerability, and functioning.

## SLIDE 2

- The background subject is treatment-resistant depression, meaning depression that persists despite correctly conducted antidepressant treatments.
- In this study, resistance is defined pragmatically: the patient has not responded to at least two antidepressant treatments during the current episode.
- The treatment rationale is dopaminergic.
- Pramipexole is an agonist of dopamine D2 and D3 receptors.
- The authors hypothesise that it could act on anhedonia, loss of motivation, and decreased reward sensitivity.
- Methodologically, we are therefore in a logic of drug repurposing: we evaluate in psychiatry a drug already used in another indication.

## SLIDE 3

- The primary objective is to compare pramipexole and placebo on the change in QIDS-SR16 score between inclusion and week 12.
- The secondary objectives are broader.
- It involves describing response and remission.
- It also involves evaluating the effect on anhedonia, anxiety, functioning, and treatment satisfaction.
- Tolerability is also an important objective.
- In the initial protocol, the authors also planned to study a cognitive mechanism related to reward sensitivity.
- The initial protocol also planned a health economic evaluation over 48 weeks.
- Orally, it must be specified that the final article published in *The Lancet Psychiatry* reports mainly the clinical results, whereas the initial protocol was broader.

## SLIDE 4

- The design is that of a classic comparative therapeutic trial, with a high level of rigour.
- The study is prospective. This means that the protocol, endpoints, and analysis are defined before knowing the results.
- The study is multicentre. This point improves generalisability compared to a single-centre trial, even if the population remains specialised.
- The trial is randomised. Randomisation serves to randomly distribute known and unknown prognostic factors between groups.
- The trial is placebo-controlled. Here, the placebo does not replace standard treatment: it is added to the antidepressant already in progress, exactly like pramipexole in the other arm.
- The study is conducted in double-blind. In theory, neither the participant nor the evaluating investigator knows which treatment is administered.
- The groups are parallel. Each participant remains in a single arm throughout the follow-up.
- The design is not adaptive. In other words, the arm structure and analysis strategy do not change during the trial based on observed results.
- It is a superiority trial. The question is not to show that pramipexole is "at least as good", but that it is better than placebo.
- The overall scheme includes two distinct times: a run-in phase before randomisation, then a treatment and follow-up phase of 48 weeks after randomisation.
- The protocol is registered, the statistical analysis plan is prespecified, and the study is sponsored by the University of Oxford.
- In practice, all this places the study at a high methodological level to answer a clinical efficacy question.

## SLIDE 5

- The trial started with an internal single-centre pilot in Oxford. This pilot mainly serves to verify that the study setup is feasible under good conditions.
- Recruitment was then extended to several NHS centres.
- In the final publication, nine English sites effectively contributed to recruitment.
- The sites are mixed: some are academic, others non-academic. This is useful, as it avoids a sample recruited solely in hyper-specialised university centres.
- Recruitment was done through several channels.
- Patients could be referred by primary care.
- They could also be referred by secondary care.
- The authors searched for patients from primary care health records.
- They also used online advertisements and posters.
- Why is this important?
- Because resistant depression does not always follow a linear care pathway. Some patients consult intermittently and can be missed by purely hospital-based recruitment.
- Despite this, all recruited participants were then evaluated and followed in secondary psychiatric services for the duration of the trial.
- Therefore, the population is not "general population", but indeed a population followed in a specialised setting.

## SLIDE 6

- The inclusion criteria define a clearly resistant population pharmacologically.
- Participants had to be aged 18 or older.
- Men and women could be included.
- The diagnosis had to be Major Depressive Disorder unipolar according to DSM-5.
- This diagnosis was not left to clinical impression alone: it was confirmed by a structured interview, the MINI version 7.0.2.
- Participants had to have a QIDS-SR16 score strictly greater than 10.
- In practice, this means the authors were not targeting mild forms, but at minimum moderate depression.
- All participants had to be already taking an antidepressant at the time of inclusion.
- Pramipexole is therefore studied as an augmentation strategy, not as monotherapy.
- Finally, participants had to have failed at least two pharmacological antidepressant treatments during the current depressive episode.
- This definition of failure is pragmatic.
- Each treatment had to have been taken for at least 4 weeks, unless tolerability prevented going that far.
- Each treatment also had to have been given at the minimum recommended dose according to the guidelines used by the authors, notably the Maudsley Prescribing Guidelines and the British National Formulary.
- We can say orally that the definition adopted is clinical and operational: it is not the most complex possible, but it is consistent with practice.

## SLIDE 7

- Exclusion criteria are important, as they also say to whom the results do not apply well.
- Patients with current or past psychosis were excluded.
- Patients with bipolar disorder were excluded.
- Patients with significant current or past difficulties with impulse control were excluded.
- Concomitant antipsychotic treatment was not allowed.
- Contraindications to pramipexole led to exclusion.
- Untreated or unstable medical conditions also led to exclusion.
- Patients with severe suicidal or homicidal risk were not included.
- Parkinson's disease was part of the exclusions cited in the flow diagram.
- The flow diagram adds very concrete examples: absence of major depression diagnosis, QIDS score too low, absence of current antidepressant, absence of failure of at least two antidepressants, taking antipsychotics, impulsivity difficulties, contraindication to treatment, or unstable medical state.
- This selection has two consequences.
- First consequence: it improves the safety of the trial and makes the comparison cleaner.
- Second consequence: it removes profiles in whom adverse dopaminergic effects could be the most frequent or most severe.
- We must therefore keep in mind that the tolerability observed here might be slightly more favourable than that observed in routine practice.

## SLIDE 8

- Before randomisation, eligible participants entered a run-in phase of 2 weeks.
- This phase is very important to understand the methodological logic of the trial.
- First objective: verify that symptoms remain stable for a short period before comparing treatments.
- Second objective: verify that participants know how to use and complete self-questionnaires regularly.
- Third objective: limit what the authors call baseline inflation.
- Baseline inflation corresponds to an artificially high initial score.
- If this phenomenon is not controlled, there is a risk of attributing to the treatment an improvement that is actually linked to an overestimated starting point.
- As the primary endpoint is a self-assessed score, repeated multiple times, this precaution is methodologically relevant.
- On the other hand, it has a cost.
- It selects participants who are more adherent, more stable, and capable of following procedures correctly.
- Therefore, it improves data quality, but it distances the sample slightly from the most disorganised real life.

## SLIDE 9

- The pre-randomisation pathway is organised in several steps.
- First, there is the screening visit.
- At that time, the diagnosis is confirmed and major eligibility criteria are checked.
- Then, the participant enters the run-in phase.
- Finally, a new evaluation is performed between 1 and 5 weeks after screening to confirm definitive eligibility for randomisation.
- This point is useful to explain to the jury.
- Screening alone is not enough: the authors want to verify that the patient remains eligible after this short observation period.
- Exclusions during the run-in show the utility of this step.
- Some participants had a QIDS that became too low.
- Others were not capable of following procedures correctly.
- Some withdrew their consent.
- Others changed antidepressants during the run-in.
- One participant started psychotherapy.
- Another presented an increase in impulsivity.
- Others were excluded for personal reasons.
- Clearly, the pre-randomisation phase serves not only to observe: it also serves to purify the population to randomise only participants who are truly comparable and capable of going to the primary endpoint.

## SLIDE 10

- Randomisation was centralised and performed online.
- This point is essential, because centralised randomisation improves allocation concealment.
- In other words, it reduces the risk that an investigator can anticipate the next assigned treatment.
- The allocation ratio was 1 to 1.
- The system used was Sortition, developed by the Primary Care Clinical Trials Unit at Oxford.
- The first ten participants were randomised without minimisation.
- The idea is simple: at the very beginning of a trial, if strict minimisation is applied immediately, the sequence can become too easy to guess.
- After these first ten cases, groups were balanced by minimisation on four prognostic variables.
- The first variable was the centre.
- The second was age, classified as 18 to 50 years versus over 50 years.
- The third was reported sex.
- The fourth was initial severity of QIDS-SR16, classified as 11 to 15, 16 to 20, or over 20.
- The process was not totally deterministic.
- There was an 80% probability of assigning the treatment that minimised the imbalance.
- This is a good strategy when the sample size is moderate.
- It improves the balance between groups without making the sequence completely predictable.

## SLIDE 11

- Blinding is another pillar of the protocol.
- Participants were blinded.
- Investigators were blinded.
- Researchers involved in recruitment and assessment visits were blinded.
- The statistician in charge of the final analysis remained blinded until this analysis.
- The only unblinded team was the central pharmacy, for practical dispensing reasons.
- The placebo was presented in capsules identical to those of the active treatment.
- Methodologically, this corresponds to high-quality blinding.
- But the authors did not stop at "theoretical" blinding.
- They also evaluated functional unblinding.
- This distinction is important to explain orally.
- A trial can be technically double-blind, while becoming partially transparent if adverse effects make one guess the treatment.
- Here, it was particularly plausible, because pramipexole has recognisable effects like nausea, headache, dizziness, or drowsiness.
- At the end of the study, participants were therefore invited to guess their group and indicate their level of certainty.
- This is a very good practice, as it allows documenting potential bias rather than ignoring it.

## SLIDE 12

- The experimental intervention is described in a very standardised way.
- It was immediate-release pramipexole.
- The medication was taken in a single dose in the evening.
- The initial dose was 0.25 mg.
- The increase was done in steps of 0.25 mg every 3 days.
- The target dose was 2.5 mg, reached in 4 weeks if tolerability permitted.
- The protocol specifies that doses are expressed as pramipexole salt.
- The choice of this target is not arbitrary.
- The authors relied on previous trials and Fawcett's case series, which suggested that some patients responded to relatively high doses.
- At the same time, they remained below the upper regulatory limit of 4.5 mg.
- The comparator was a matched placebo, administered according to exactly the same procedure.
- In method, this is important: when both arms follow the same intake and adaptation sequence, bias linked to simply being "more treated" in one group than in the other is limited.

## SLIDE 13

- The protocol also details the procedure in case of intolerance, which is a real strength.
- If adverse effects appeared, the dose had to be lowered to the maximum tolerated dose.
- Once this tolerated dose was identified, the patient remained at this dose for the rest of the study.
- The protocol did not plan for new re-titration afterwards.
- In case of reduction, tapering was done in steps every 3 days.
- The authors explain that this gradual reduction aimed to limit the risk of dopamine agonist withdrawal syndrome.
- This is an interesting point orally, because it shows that the study does not just test "a drug yes or no".
- It precisely frames how to introduce it, adapt it, and stop it.
- In practice, this improves safety and makes treatment exposure more homogeneous.

## SLIDE 14

- The studied treatment was always added to the background antidepressant.
- In other words, pramipexole was never used as a replacement for the current antidepressant.
- This is important, because the exact methodological question is not "is pramipexole a good antidepressant?", but "does adding pramipexole to an antidepressant already in place bring additional benefit?"
- Before week 12, changes to the background antidepressant were not allowed, except for clinical necessity.
- After week 12, they became possible.
- This point changes the interpretation significantly.
- During the first 12 weeks, co-interventions are strongly controlled.
- The estimated effect is therefore cleaner causally.
- After 12 weeks, the study becomes more pragmatic.
- It moves closer to what happens in real life, where clinicians adjust treatments.
- On the other hand, causal interpretation becomes less pure, as the contrast between groups also depends on other therapeutic modifications.
- The initial protocol also specifies that the team monitored adherence and treatment difficulties during regular telephone calls.


## SLIDE 15

- The architecture of endpoints is broad and consistent with the ambition of the trial.
- There is a primary endpoint focused on efficacy at 12 weeks.
- There are several clinical secondary endpoints, which explore other dimensions of potential benefit.
- There are also several safety endpoints, which is essential with a dopamine agonist.
- In the initial protocol, a mechanistic component and a health economic component were also planned.
- This means that the trial seeks to answer several questions in parallel.
- First question: does the drug improve symptoms?
- Second question: does it act on specific dimensions like anhedonia or functioning?
- Third question: is it tolerable?
- Fourth question: can we understand a plausible mechanism of action and a broader impact on quality of life and costs?
- It is therefore a clinical trial, but with an ambition slightly broader than a simple symptomatic trial.

## SLIDE 16

- The primary endpoint is the change in total QIDS-SR16 score between baseline and week 12.
- This choice is explicitly justified by the authors.
- The QIDS-SR16 is a validated tool, with good psychometric properties.
- It is easy to repeat over time.
- It is self-administered, which facilitates frequent follow-up.
- It is focused on depressive symptoms.
- It is already widely used in trials in psychiatry.
- Concretely, the QIDS-SR16 comprises 16 items covering 9 symptomatic domains.
- The total score ranges from 0 to 27.
- Item 12 also allows identifying suicidal ideation.
- Why is this choice methodologically interesting?
- Because it allows repeated, low-cost, standardised measurements directly focused on the patient's experience.
- Why can this choice also be discussed?
- Because a self-assessed endpoint is more vulnerable to an expectation bias if the patient guesses their treatment.
- This is exactly the reason why the question of functional unblinding becomes so important in this trial.

## SLIDE 17

- The final article reports several clinical secondary endpoints, and they must be classified well to remain clear orally.
- First, there are the other measures of depressive efficacy.
- We find the QIDS-SR16 at 48 weeks.
- We also find the response at 12 weeks, defined by a decrease of more than 50% from the initial score.
- We find remission at 12 weeks, defined by a QIDS-SR16 score less than or equal to 5.
- Next, there are complementary clinical measures.
- The QIDS-C provides a clinician version of depressive severity.
- The SHAPS measures anhedonia.
- The GAD-7 measures anxiety.
- The WSAS measures functioning.
- The TSQM-9 measures patient satisfaction with treatment.
- Finally, there are safety endpoints.
- They include adverse events, serious adverse events, discontinuation of treatment for intolerance, QUIP-RS for impulse control disorders, ALTMAN for manic symptoms, and suicidality extracted from item 12 of QIDS-SR16.
- This structure is useful to explain, because it shows that the evaluation does not just focus on "more or less depression", but also on functional experience, acceptability, and specific risks of the molecule.

## SLIDE 18

- The schedule of assessments is dense, and this is a real strength of the trial.
- The QIDS-SR16 is collected every week from screening to week 12.
- Then, it is collected every 4 weeks up to 48 weeks.
- The QIDS-C is performed at all study visits.
- The ALTMAN is collected every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks.
- The GAD-7 follows the same frequency.
- The SHAPS is collected every 2 weeks up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks.
- The QUIP-RS is collected every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks.
- The WSAS is collected every 4 weeks up to 12 weeks, then at weeks 24, 36, and 48.
- The TSQM-9 is collected at weeks 0, 6, 12, 24, and 48.
- Most of these measures are entered via the True Colours platform.
- In parallel, a research assistant calls participants at screening, then every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks.
- These calls serve to document adverse effects, suicidality, impulsivity, treatment modifications, and adherence.
- Why is this methodologically useful?
- Because frequent follow-up improves the quality of longitudinal data.
- Because it reduces the risk of missing an important adverse event.
- And because it allows better distinguishing a true therapeutic effect from a variation that is too temporary or poorly documented.

## SLIDE 19

- The initial protocol also included a mechanistic component focused on reward sensitivity.
- This is not the heart of the final article, but it helps to understand the general biological hypothesis of the trial.
- The authors used a probabilistic decision task.
- The task comprised 3 blocks of 60 trials, i.e., 180 trials in total.
- There were gain trials and loss trials.
- The participant had to learn progressively from the results obtained.
- Reward sensitivity was then estimated using a reinforcement learning model.
- These measurements were performed at the randomisation visit, then at 2 weeks and at 12 weeks.
- Why is this component interesting?
- Because it links the clinical question to a plausible cognitive mechanism.
- The authors do not just ask if the drug works.
- They also ask if it acts on a relevant theoretical process, and if this process could help predict response.
- In the final article, this component is announced but detailed results are reported elsewhere.

## SLIDE 20

- Analysis populations must be explained precisely, as this is often a point of question from the jury.
- In the final article, the main population corresponds to all randomised and eligible participants.
- One participant was withdrawn after randomisation because they turned out to be ineligible.
- This means that in practice, the main analysis falls under a modified ITT, and not a strict ITT in the sense of "all randomised without exception".
- Efficacy and safety analyses rely on this same set.
- The main text does not highlight a true per-protocol population.
- This is important, because it means the article privileges a pragmatic logic: patients are analysed according to their randomisation group, independently of perfect adherence.
- The initial protocol did announce that primary and secondary efficacy analyses would be performed by intention to treat.
- On the other hand, it also specified that mechanistic and economic analyses would be limited to subjects having the required data, without imputation.
- One must therefore remember that there is no single universal population for all the questions of the trial.

## SLIDE 21

- The sample size calculation relies on an explicit clinical hypothesis, which is a good point.
- The authors considered that a difference of 3 points on the QIDS-SR16 at 12 weeks would be clinically relevant.
- The assumed standard deviation was 5.4.
- This corresponded to a standardised effect size of about 0.56.
- The targeted power was 90%.
- The alpha retained for the primary clinical objective was 5%.
- In this framework, 68 patients per group were necessary if one did not take into account lost to follow-up.
- In the initial protocol, with 20% anticipated lost to follow-up, the planned total was 170 participants for the clinical objective alone.
- If one wanted to additionally protect the alpha risk for the mechanistic component, the total went up to 204.
- In the final article, the authors explain that they adjusted this estimate because the actual observed rate of lost to follow-up was lower, around 8%.
- The estimated need for the primary endpoint was therefore revised to 74 patients per group, i.e., 148 in total.
- They add that by also taking into account the mechanistic component with an alpha lowered to 2.5%, 89 patients per group would have been needed, i.e., 178 in total.
- This point deserves a simple explanation orally.
- It is not about changing the primary endpoint along the way.
- It is about updating a logistical hypothesis on lost to follow-up based on what was actually observed.

## SLIDE 22

- The main statistical analysis relies on a generalised mixed model including all QIDS-SR16 data collected up to week 48.
- Fixed effects include treatment group, timepoint, the interaction between time and treatment group, as well as minimisation variables.
- Treatment site is included as a random effect and participant identification as a random intercept.
- In practice, this type of model is well adapted when one has repeated measures in the same patients, modelling intra-individual correlation.
- The subtle methodological point is the following.
- The primary endpoint is defined at 12 weeks.
- However, the article specifies that the model uses all QIDS-SR16 data collected up to 48 weeks.
- In the initial protocol, the description gave rather the impression of an analysis focused on weekly measures up to 12 weeks.
- In the final publication, the effect estimated at 12 weeks is therefore extracted from a broader longitudinal model.
- This strategy is defensible, as it can improve the efficiency of estimation and better use available information.
- But it is a point that deserves to be explained orally.
- Indeed, part of the data used for modelling comes from a period where concomitant treatments are less strictly controlled.

## SLIDE 23

- Secondary analyses are chosen according to the type of variable studied.
- For continuous variables, the authors report adjusted mean differences.
- For binary variables, they use a mixed logistic model then express results in the form of relative risks.
- For count data, they report incidence ratios.
- Adjustments include randomised group, age, sex, baseline QIDS-SR16 score, time by group interaction, and sometimes the initial value of the considered criterion.
- The site is treated as a random effect.
- For longitudinal data, the participant is taken into account as a random intercept.
- Orally, two notions must be made explicit.
- A fixed effect corresponds to a factor whose average influence in the model we want to estimate directly.
- A random effect serves rather to take into account a source of structured variability, for example between subjects or between centres, without estimating a specific coefficient for each unit.
- This distinction is important, as it explains why the authors can both adjust the analysis and respect the real structure of the data.

## SLIDE 24

- The management of missing data deserves a cautious discussion, as it can influence interpretation.
- The main text does not describe simple imputation of the last observation carried forward type.
- This is rather reassuring, as this type of method is often judged too simplifying.
- The main analysis relies on a mixed model using available data.
- In practice, this amounts to assuming that missing data are compatible with a MAR type hypothesis, i.e., missing at random conditionally on the variables integrated into the model.
- Put differently, a datum can be missing for reasons linked to information already observed, but not for a reason entirely hidden and independent of what the model already knows.
- For response and remission at 12 weeks, the authors explicitly specify that there was no imputation.
- Only participants having a datum available in the planned window are therefore counted in these analyses.
- Sensitivity analyses are mentioned, but their details are referred to the appendix.
- The critical message to retain is simple.
- The main strategy is standard and acceptable.
- On the other hand, the body of the article does not allow discussing finely the robustness to more unfavourable scenarios, for example if missing data were not MAR.

## SLIDE 25

- Some operational points must still be mentioned, as they influence the interpretation of the long phase.
- The authors specify that no endpoint was modified after the start of the study.
- On the other hand, the lost to follow-up parameter used in the sample size calculation was adjusted.
- Recruitment was delayed by the COVID-19 pandemic.
- Participants included after September 2023 had shorter follow-up.
- All nevertheless had sufficient time to reach week 12, which corresponds to the primary endpoint.
- The methodological consequence is therefore quite clear.
- The main result at 12 weeks is not really weakened by this point.
- On the other hand, analyses at 48 weeks relate to a more restricted subset of participants.
- The long term must therefore be presented as informative, but methodologically less robust than the main analysis.

## SLIDE 26

- The flow of participants must be presented clearly.
- 217 participants were seen at screening.
- 66 were excluded before analytical inclusion.
- 151 were randomised.
- One participant was then withdrawn after randomisation because they were ineligible.
- In the end, 150 participants were analysed, i.e., 75 per group.
- For the primary endpoint, data available at 12 weeks concerned 68 participants out of 75 in the pramipexole group and 67 out of 75 in the placebo group.
- At 48 weeks, only 40 participants on pramipexole and 39 on placebo had reached this follow-up duration before closure.
- Orally, several situations must be distinguished well.
- There are missing data at the time of the primary endpoint.
- There is discontinuation of treatment.
- There is complete withdrawal from the study.
- And there is the fact of not having been able to reach 48 weeks before the closure of the trial.


## SLIDE 27

- Initial characteristics confirm that this is a severe population.
- The mean age is 44.9 years.
- 56% of participants are women.
- The mean QIDS-SR16 score is 16.3, which corresponds to severe depression on average.
- The median duration of the current episode is 8.5 years.
- The mean number of ineffective treatments during the current episode is 3.5.
- About 21% of participants were already receiving a pharmacological augmentation strategy at the time of randomisation.
- In practice, this describes a clinically heavy population.
- The disease is chronic.
- Therapeutic resistance is marked.
- Functional impact is significant.
- This element reinforces the clinical relevance of the observed signal.

## SLIDE 28

- The main result at 12 weeks is clear.
- The QIDS-SR16 score decreases on average by 6.4 points in the pramipexole group.
- In the placebo group, the decrease is 2.4 points.
- The adjusted mean difference between groups is -3.91 points.
- The 95% confidence interval goes from -5.37 to -2.45.
- The p-value is less than 0.0001.
- This result is important at three levels.
- First, it is statistically very significant.
- Second, it is compatible with clinical relevance, since the threshold retained for sample size calculation was 3 points.
- Finally, the standardised effect size is large, around 0.87.
- We can therefore say orally that this is not just a weak statistical signal.
- The observed gap exceeds the threshold considered a priori as clinically important.

## SLIDE 29

- Secondary results at 12 weeks go in the same direction.
- The response rate is 44% under pramipexole versus 16% under placebo.
- The relative risk is 2.72.
- The remission rate is 28% under pramipexole versus 8% under placebo.
- The relative risk is 3.94.
- The authors also observe an improvement in clinician QIDS.
- They also observe an improvement in SHAPS, therefore in anhedonia.
- The GAD-7 also improves, which suggests a benefit on anxiety.
- From a methodological point of view, this consistency between several endpoints is important.
- It makes the idea of an isolated or aberrant result less likely.
- The fact that the clinician QIDS evolves in the same direction as the self-assessed QIDS reinforces the credibility of the signal a bit, even if it does not remove the problem of unblinding.

## SLIDE 30

- At 48 weeks, results remain globally in favour of pramipexole.
- The authors report a maintenance of benefit on QIDS-SR16, with an adjusted difference of -2.02.
- They also report an improvement in WSAS.
- Treatment satisfaction is also higher.
- However, one must remain cautious in interpretation.
- Not all participants had 48 full weeks of follow-up.
- After 12 weeks, antidepressant modifications were allowed.
- These modifications were more frequent in the placebo group.
- The rigorous message is therefore the following.
- The maintenance signal is interesting.
- On the other hand, the level of causal evidence is stronger at 12 weeks than at 48 weeks.

## SLIDE 31

- Tolerability is a major element of interpretation.
- Almost all participants reported at least one adverse event.
- Discontinuation for intolerance concerned 20% of patients under pramipexole versus 5% under placebo.
- The relative risk is 3.95.
- Frequent adverse effects are consistent with the known profile of the molecule.
- The most frequent are nausea, headache, dizziness, drowsiness, sleep disorders, and vomiting.
- The authors also monitored more specific signals.
- These were impulse control disorders, manic symptoms, psychosis, and suicidal ideation.
- Two cases of impulse control disorders were observed under pramipexole.
- These disorders regressed after dose reduction.

## SLIDE 32

- The main methodological limitations must be exposed clearly.
- The primary endpoint is self-assessed.
- There is significant functional unblinding at the end of the study.
- One participant was excluded after randomisation, which leads to a modified ITT.
- Details of sensitivity analyses are not provided in the body of the text.
- Patient selection is significant.
- Ethnicity data are not available.
- Unblinding deserves a specific discussion.
- 77% of placebo group participants and 71% of pramipexole group participants correctly guessed their group.
- The difference is statistically significant, with p < 0.001.
- As the primary endpoint relies on a self-questionnaire, this unblinding can increase an expectation effect.
- This is probably the main threat to the internal validity of the study.

## SLIDE 33

- Despite these limitations, the study has numerous methodological strengths.
- It is a randomised, multicentre, placebo-controlled trial.
- Allocation is centralised.
- The statistical analysis plan is prespecified.
- The run-in phase reduces the risk of baseline inflation.
- Co-interventions are strictly framed up to 12 weeks.
- Longitudinal follow-up is fine, thanks to the digital platform and regular calls.
- The different efficacy endpoints are globally consistent with each other.
- Finally, the population is truly resistant, which gives a real clinical interest to the question asked.
- In summary, global internal validity is good.
- The main result is credible.
- On the other hand, the trial is not totally protected against the consequences of imperfect blinding.

## SLIDE 34

- External validity is correct, but it has limitations.
- Results are relevant for adults with treatment-resistant unipolar depression, followed in specialised psychiatry in the UK.
- On the other hand, extrapolation is more delicate towards general practice.
- It is also more delicate in patients with severe comorbidities, bipolar disorder, psychosis, or history of impulsivity.
- Furthermore, tolerability might be less favourable outside the trial than in this very selected framework.
- Clinically, pramipexole therefore appears as a plausible augmentation option.
- On the other hand, it cannot be presented as a definitive new standard at this stage.
- Direct comparative trials would be useful, for example against quetiapine or lithium, with particular attention paid to tolerability and cost-effectiveness.

## SLIDE 35

- The conclusion must remain cautious.
- The study shows convincingly that at 12 weeks, in patients with treatment-resistant unipolar depression already treated with an antidepressant, the addition of pramipexole improves symptoms more than a placebo.
- The observed signal is strong, consistent, and clinically plausible.
- On the other hand, tolerability is poorer.
- The level of confidence is therefore good for short-term efficacy in this selected population.
- It is more moderate for generalisation to broader populations.
- It is also more moderate for the very long-term effect.
- Finally, it is more moderate for the exact magnitude of the effect if blinding was imperfect.
- A possible concluding sentence orally would be the following.
- This study supports the use of pramipexole as an augmentation option in certain resistant patients, but on condition of selecting patients rigorously, monitoring tolerability closely, and not overinterpreting the scope of the result.

## Possible jury questions

### Question 1

Why is placebo an acceptable comparator here?

Answer: because there is no, in this precise situation, pharmacological augmentation strategy unanimously recognised as a first-line standard. The placebo therefore allows cleanly isolating the additive effect of pramipexole on the background antidepressant treatment.

### Question 2

Why is the run-in phase methodologically interesting?

Answer: it allows verifying adherence to data collection, ensuring that symptoms are stable, and limiting baseline inflation, i.e., an artificial exaggeration of the initial score. In return, it selects subjects who are more stable and more adherent.

### Question 3

What is meant by baseline inflation?

Answer: it is an artificially high starting score, for example due to the selection context or patient expectations. If this phenomenon is not controlled, one can overestimate the improvement observed afterwards.

### Question 4

Is the study really intention-to-treat?

Answer: not in the strict sense. The authors use the term ITT, but a randomised participant was withdrawn after randomisation because they turned out to be ineligible. In practice, we therefore speak rather of a modified ITT.

### Question 5

Why is randomisation by minimisation interesting here?

Answer: because with a moderate sample size and several centres, it limits imbalances on important prognostic factors such as age, sex, centre, and initial severity. It is particularly useful when one wants to obtain comparable groups without having a very large sample size.

### Question 6

Did the double blind really work?

Answer: it probably worked well initially technically, but it was functionally imperfect during the study. The adverse effects of pramipexole probably helped participants guess their group. This is problematic especially because the primary endpoint is self-assessed.

### Question 7

Why can the choice of QIDS-SR16 as primary endpoint be discussed?

Answer: it is a validated tool, sensitive to change, and easy to repeat during follow-up. On the other hand, as it is self-assessed, it is more vulnerable to an expectation bias than a purely rater-based or more objective endpoint.

### Question 8

How did the authors manage missing data?

Answer: for the main analysis, they use a mixed model on available data, which corresponds in practice to a valid approach under MAR hypothesis. For response and remission at 12 weeks, they specify that there was no imputation. Full details of sensitivity analyses are referred to the appendix.

### Question 9

What is a MAR hypothesis?

Answer: this means that the probability that a datum is missing can depend on information already observed, but not on the true unobserved value once this information is taken into account in the model. It is a frequent hypothesis, but it is not directly verifiable.

### Question 10

Why is it a bit particular to use all data up to 48 weeks to estimate a main effect at 12 weeks?

Answer: because the primary endpoint is defined at 12 weeks, while the model also borrows information from other measurement times. This strategy can improve precision, but it deserves to be made explicit, as the period after 12 weeks is less controlled.

### Question 11

Are the secondary analyses solid?

Answer: they are globally well adapted and consistent with the main analysis. On the other hand, they remain secondary. They reinforce the consistency of the signal, but they do not individually have the same demonstrative strength as the primary endpoint.

### Question 12

Why are the results at 48 weeks less convincing causally?

Answer: because part of the participants could not reach 48 weeks before closure, and because after 12 weeks antidepressants could be modified. The contrast between groups therefore becomes less pure.

### Question 13

What to think of the benefit-risk ratio?

Answer: it seems interesting in very resistant patients, because the symptomatic gain is clear. On the other hand, this benefit comes with a non-negligible cost in tolerability, with more treatment discontinuations and dopaminergic effects that require monitoring.

### Question 14

What is the main strength of internal validity?

Answer: it is the combination of centralised randomisation, a placebo comparator, a prespecified protocol, strict control of co-interventions up to 12 weeks, and dense longitudinal follow-up.

### Question 15

What is the main limitation of external validity?

Answer: the population is very selected and specialised. In particular, certain high-risk profiles, such as patients with history of impulsivity, psychosis, or bipolarity, were excluded. Therefore, one cannot transpose the results without caution.

### Question 16

Why is having a clinician QIDS also useful?

Answer: because it brings a different source of evaluation from the self-questionnaire alone. The fact that the clinician QIDS evolves in the same direction reinforces the credibility of the signal a bit, even if it does not completely settle the problem of unblinding.

### Question 17

What does this study really show, and what does it not show?

Answer: it shows a short-term clinical benefit of pramipexole as an adjunct to an antidepressant in selected patients having resistant unipolar depression. On the other hand, it does not yet show that pramipexole is the best augmentation strategy, nor that the benefit observed is generalisable to all patients, nor that it is perfectly durable in the long term.
