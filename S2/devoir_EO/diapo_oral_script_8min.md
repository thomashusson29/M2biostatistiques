

## Slide 1

So, I am going to present a randomised clinical trial about pramipexole augmentation in treatment-resistant unipolar depression. I will focus mainly on the methodology, then on the main results and their interpretation.

## Slide 2

This article was published in *Lancet Psychiatry* in 2025. It is a phase three, multicentre, double-blind, placebo-controlled randomised trial conducted in nine centres in England. The objective was to test whether pramipexole, added to ongoing antidepressant treatment, improved depressive symptoms better than placebo in adults with treatment-resistant unipolar depression.

## Slide 3

Pramipexole is a selective dopamine D2 and D3 receptor agonist. It is different from standard antidepressants, which mainly target serotonin. It may improve anhedonia and low motivation. Before this trial, the evidence was promising but still limited, with only small randomised trials and observational studies.

## Slide 4

This slide shows the overall design. Eligible participants had major depressive disorder, a QIDS self-report score above ten, ongoing antidepressant treatment, and failure of at least two antidepressants during the current episode. After a two-week run-in phase, they were randomised one-to-one to pramipexole or placebo. The primary endpoint was change in QIDS score at week twelve.

## Slide 5

This slide focuses on how participants were identified and assessed. Participants were recruited and managed in local secondary care services. They had to be taking an antidepressant that they were willing to continue. Treatment resistance meant failure of at least two adequate antidepressant trials during the current episode.

## Slide 6

I am showing the design again here in a simpler visual way. The important point is that this was not a trial in general depression. It was a trial in a selected population with treatment-resistant unipolar depression, which makes the question clinically relevant.

## Slide 7

Now I will focus on randomisation and masking. Randomisation was centralised and performed through an online system, with one-to-one allocation. The authors used minimisation to balance the groups on site, age, sex, and baseline depression severity. The first ten participants were randomised without minimisation, and after that the best balancing allocation was chosen with an eighty percent probability. This strengthens group comparability and allocation concealment.

## Slide 8

This slide returns to the run-in phase. Before randomisation, the investigators checked symptom stability, compliance with self-rating scales, and final eligibility. This step reduced the risk of enrolling unstable or poorly documented cases.

## Slide 9

This slide shows the treatment procedures. Pramipexole started at zero point two five milligrams per day and was increased every three days toward two point five milligrams over four weeks, if tolerated. If adverse effects occurred, the dose could be reduced. Background antidepressant treatment was kept stable before week twelve unless change was clinically necessary.

## Slide 10

Again, this slide is a reminder that the intervention was pragmatic, because pramipexole was added to routine antidepressant treatment, but the early phase of the protocol remained controlled enough to preserve internal validity.

## Slide 11

The primary outcome was change from baseline in the QIDS self-report score at week twelve. This outcome is clinically relevant, but it is subjective because it is self-rated. Secondary outcomes included response, remission, clinician-rated depression, anhedonia, anxiety, functioning, treatment satisfaction, and several safety measures.

## Slide 12

For the sample size calculation, the authors chose a clinically important difference of three points on the QIDS self-report scale. They assumed a standard deviation of five point four, which gave a standardised effect size of zero point five six. They used ninety percent power and a two-sided alpha risk of five percent. Seventy-five participants per group were randomised.

## Slide 13

The primary analysis used a generalised linear mixed-effects model. The model included all available repeated QIDS measurements up to week forty-eight. Fixed effects included treatment group, timepoint, the interaction between treatment and time, and the minimisation variables. Site was a random effect, and participant was a random intercept.

## Slide 14

Other outcomes were analysed with models adapted to their type. Continuous outcomes were analysed with mixed-effects linear regression. Binary outcomes were analysed with mixed-effects logistic regression. Count outcomes were analysed with a mixed-effects Poisson model. The supplementary appendix also reports sensitivity analyses for missing data, outliers, and departures from the missing-at-random assumption.

## Slide 15

Before looking at the results, it is important to understand follow-up completeness. At week twelve, primary outcome data were available for sixty-eight of seventy-five participants in the pramipexole group and sixty-seven of seventy-five in the placebo group. Missing data were therefore limited for the primary endpoint. At week forty-eight, only about forty participants per group had reached follow-up.

## Slide 16

At week twelve, the observed mean improvement in QIDS score was minus six point four in the pramipexole group and minus two point four in the placebo group. The adjusted mean difference was minus three point nine one, with a confidence interval from minus five point three seven to minus two point four five. The standardised mean difference was minus zero point eight seven, which is a large effect size.

## Slide 17

The benefit appeared to persist over time. At week forty-eight, the adjusted mean difference for QIDS was minus two point zero two. However, this longer-term interpretation must be more cautious because follow-up was incomplete.

## Slide 18

In terms of safety, the proportion of participants with at least one adverse event was high in both groups. However, tolerability was worse with pramipexole. Discontinuation due to adverse effects was more frequent in the pramipexole group, with twenty percent versus five percent in the placebo group. Some adverse effects were also more recognisable with pramipexole, especially nausea, headache, dizziness, vomiting, and somnolence.

## Slide 19

This leads to an important methodological limitation, which is functional unblinding. Many participants correctly guessed their treatment allocation, about three quarters overall. This matters because the primary endpoint was self-rated, so expectancy effects may have inflated part of the observed benefit.

## Slide 20

The main strengths of the trial are the robust design, the clinically relevant treatment-resistant population, the central randomisation with minimisation, and the appropriate mixed-model analysis. The main limitations are functional unblinding, the self-rated primary outcome, moderate sample size for some safety questions, limited external validity, and more uncertainty in the long-term results.

## Slide 21

In conclusion, this study shows that pramipexole augmentation was more effective than placebo at week twelve in treatment-resistant unipolar depression. The efficacy signal was clinically meaningful and was supported by several secondary outcomes. However, tolerability was worse, and functional unblinding is an important methodological limitation. Overall, pramipexole appears promising, but the results should still be interpreted with caution.
