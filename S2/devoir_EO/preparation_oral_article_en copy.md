# Oral preparation on the article

## SLIDE 1

- Article presented: trial on pramipexole as an adjunct to an antidepressant in patients with treatment-resistant unipolar depression.
- Article title: pramipexole augmentation in the acute phase of treatment-resistant unipolar depression, a randomised placebo-controlled trial, double-blind, in the UK.
- The research question is very clinical: in adults already treated with an antidepressant but insufficiently improved, does the addition of pramipexole reduce symptoms more than a placebo?
- The primary endpoint focuses on efficacy at 12 weeks.
- The study also follows patients up to 48 weeks for maintenance, tolerability, and functioning.

## SLIDE 2

- The theme is treatment-resistant depression, meaning depression that persists despite well-conducted treatments.
- In this study, resistance is defined pragmatically: lack of response to at least two antidepressant treatments during the current episode.
- The pharmacological rationale is dopaminergic.
- Pramipexole is a D2/D3 agonist.
- The idea is that it could act on anhedonia, decreased motivation, and reduced reward sensitivity.
- Methodological interest: we are in a logic of drug repurposing, i.e., testing in psychiatry a drug already known in another indication.

## SLIDE 3

- Primary objective: compare pramipexole versus placebo on the change in QIDS-SR16 between baseline and week 12.
- Secondary objectives:
- describe response and remission;
- evaluate the effect on anhedonia, anxiety, functioning, and satisfaction;
- document tolerability;
- in the initial protocol, also study a cognitive mechanism around reward sensitivity;
- add a health economic evaluation at 48 weeks.
- Important point for the oral presentation:
- the main article published in *The Lancet Psychiatry* reports mainly the clinical part;
- the initial protocol was broader, with a mechanistic and economic component.

## SLIDE 4

- Study type:
- prospective trial;
- multicentre;
- randomised;
- placebo-controlled;
- double-blind;
- parallel-group;
- non-adaptive;
- superiority.
- There is a pre-randomisation run-in phase, then a post-randomisation treatment phase of 48 weeks.
- The protocol is registered, the analysis plan is prespecified, and the study is sponsored by the University of Oxford.
- For the jury, it must be stated that the targeted level of methodological evidence is high.

## SLIDE 5

- Practical framework of the study:
- first an internal single-centre pilot in Oxford;
- then extension to several NHS centres;
- a total of nine sites in England in the final article.
- Recruitment via several channels:
- referral by primary and secondary care;
- search in primary care records;
- online advertisements and posters.
- This diversity of recruitment has an interest:
- it reduces the bias of purely hospital-based recruitment;
- but it still keeps a very specialised population because all patients are then followed in secondary psychiatric care.
- The authors justify the use of advertising by the fact that patients with treatment-resistant depression often consult intermittently.

## SLIDE 6

- Target population:
- adults aged 18 or older;
- men and women;
- DSM-5 diagnosis of major unipolar depressive disorder confirmed by MINI version 7.0.2;
- QIDS-SR16 score greater than 10, thus at least moderate depression;
- current antidepressant treatment;
- failure of at least two pharmacological antidepressant treatments during the current episode.
- Operational definition of failure:
- at least 4 weeks of treatment;
- minimum recommended dose according to the guidelines used by the authors.
- This definition is not the most sophisticated possible, but it is defensible and pragmatic.

## SLIDE 7

- Main exclusion criteria:
- current or past psychosis;
- bipolar disorder;
- significant current or past difficulties with impulse control;
- concomitant antipsychotic treatment;
- contraindication to pramipexole;
- untreated or unstable medical condition;
- severe suicidal or homicidal risk;
- Parkinson's disease.
- The flow diagram also specifies several reasons for ineligibility.
- To say during the oral presentation:
- this selection protects safety and internal validity;
- but it removes precisely the patients in whom certain dopaminergic adverse effects would be most concerning.
- Therefore, the tolerability observed here might be slightly better than what would be seen in routine practice.

## SLIDE 8

- The run-in phase lasts 2 weeks.
- It has several roles:
- verify symptomatic stability;
- verify the ability to complete self-questionnaires;
- reduce artificial inflation of the baseline score, what is called baseline inflation.
- Baseline inflation corresponds to artificially high initial scores, which can give the illusion of improvement afterwards.
- Methodologically, it is a good idea when the primary endpoint is repeated and self-assessed.
- But this phase also selects patients who are more adherent and more stable.
- Therefore, it improves the cleanliness of the data, at the cost of lower representativeness.

## SLIDE 9

- Practical procedure before randomisation:
- screening visit;
- diagnostic confirmation;
- verification of eligibility criteria;
- entry into run-in;
- new evaluation 1 to 5 weeks after screening to confirm eligibility for randomisation.
- Patients were excluded during this run-in.
- The reasons found are very instructive:
- QIDS became too low;
- impossibility to follow procedures;
- withdrawal of consent;
- change of antidepressant;
- start of psychotherapy;
- increase in impulsivity;
- personal reasons.
- This shows that the randomised population is even more selected than the simply screened population.

## SLIDE 10

- Randomisation:
- centralised;
- online;
- 1:1 ratio;
- ensured by the Oxford Sortition system.
- The first ten participants are randomised without minimisation.
- Then, groups are balanced by minimisation on four variables:
- centre;
- age, 18 to 50 years versus over 50 years;
- self-reported sex;
- initial severity of QIDS-SR16, 11 to 15, 16 to 20, or over 20.
- The process is not completely deterministic.
- There is an 80% probability of going towards the allocation that minimises imbalance.
- This improves the balance between groups without making the sequence too predictable.

## SLIDE 11

- Blinding:
- participants blinded;
- investigators blinded;
- raters blinded;
- final statistician blinded;
- central pharmacy team unblinded for dispensing.
- The placebo is matched to the active treatment.
- Therefore, on paper, the blinding arrangement is robust.
- But the authors were right to evaluate functional unblinding.
- Why?
- because recognisable adverse effects, such as nausea or drowsiness, can make one guess the treatment even if the blind is technically correct.
- At the end of the study, they ask participants to guess their group and their level of certainty.
- It is a good practice, because it allows discussing an expectation bias.

## SLIDE 12

- Experimental intervention:
- immediate-release pramipexole;
- single intake in the evening;
- initial dose 0.25 mg;
- increase of 0.25 mg every 3 days;
- target 2.5 mg in 4 weeks if tolerability permits.
- The protocol specifies that doses are expressed as pramipexole salt.
- The target was chosen based on previous trials and a case series having suggested a possible response at relatively high doses.
- It remains below the regulatory upper limit of 4.5 mg.
- The comparator is a matched placebo, administered according to the same procedure.

## SLIDE 13

- Management of tolerability and adaptations:
- in case of adverse effects, decrease to the maximum tolerated dose;
- maintenance then at this tolerated dose;
- no new re-titration planned;
- if dose decrease, tapering by steps every 3 days.
- The protocol justifies this gradual decrease to limit the risk of dopamine agonist withdrawal syndrome.
- This level of detail is interesting to mention at the oral presentation:
- the protocol does not just test a drug;
- it also plans how to manage exposure, adherence, and discontinuation.

## SLIDE 14

- Concomitant treatments:
- pramipexole is always added to the background antidepressant;
- before 12 weeks, no change of antidepressant except for clinical necessity;
- after 12 weeks, changes allowed.
- This is a major methodological point.
- Before 12 weeks, the estimated effect is cleaner, because co-interventions are limited.
- After 12 weeks, we enter a more pragmatic phase, closer to real life, but less causally pure.
- The initial protocol also specifies that the team monitors adherence and treatment issues during regular phone contacts.

## SLIDE 15

- Architecture of endpoints:
- a primary efficacy endpoint at 12 weeks;
- several clinical secondary endpoints;
- several safety endpoints;
- in the protocol, a mechanistic and health economic set.
- This point is important for the jury:
- the trial is not just an efficacy trial;
- it is a trial that also seeks to understand how the drug might act, and what it costs or brings in quality of life.

## SLIDE 16

- Primary endpoint:
- change in total QIDS-SR16 score between baseline and week 12.
- Why this endpoint?
- validated tool;
- easy to repeat;
- self-administered;
- focused on depressive symptoms;
- already widely used in trials in psychiatry.
- Useful detail:
- the QIDS-SR16 is a 16-item questionnaire covering 9 symptomatic domains;
- total score from 0 to 27;
- item 12 also captures suicidal ideation.
- Limitation:
- as it is a self-questionnaire, it is more exposed than a rater-based endpoint to an expectation bias if the patient guesses their group.

## SLIDE 17

- Clinical secondary endpoints reported in the final article:
- QIDS-SR16 at 48 weeks;
- response at 12 weeks, defined by a decrease of more than 50% from the initial score;
- remission at 12 weeks, defined by a QIDS-SR16 score less than or equal to 5;
- QIDS-C, thus clinician version;
- SHAPS for anhedonia;
- GAD-7 for anxiety;
- WSAS for functioning;
- TSQM-9 for satisfaction.
- Safety endpoints:
- adverse events and serious adverse events;
- discontinuation of treatment for intolerance;
- QUIP-RS for impulse control disorders;
- ALTMAN for manic symptoms;
- suicidality extracted from item 12 of QIDS-SR16.

## SLIDE 18

- Measurement schedule, and this is where the methodology becomes really rich:
- QIDS-SR16 every week from screening to week 12, then every 4 weeks up to 48 weeks;
- QIDS-C at all study visits;
- ALTMAN every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks;
- GAD-7 every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks;
- SHAPS every 2 weeks up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks;
- QUIP-RS every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks;
- WSAS every 4 weeks up to 12 weeks, then weeks 24, 36, and 48;
- TSQM-9 at weeks 0, 6, 12, 24, and 48.
- Questionnaires are mostly entered via the True Colours platform.
- In parallel, a research assistant calls participants at selection, then every week up to 12 weeks, then every 4 weeks.
- During these calls, adverse effects, suicidality, impulsivity, treatment changes, and adherence are documented.
- This is a real strength of follow-up.

## SLIDE 19

- A very fine point of method:
- the initial protocol included a mechanistic component of reward sensitivity.
- It used a probabilistic decision task:
- 3 blocks of 60 trials, i.e., 180 trials;
- gain and loss trials;
- progressive learning from feedbacks;
- estimation of reward sensitivity by a reinforcement learning model.
- Measurements at the randomisation visit, at 2 weeks, and at 12 weeks.
- Why is it interesting to say?
- because it shows that the study does not just look for an average efficacy;
- it also looks for a plausible mechanism and potentially predictors of response.
- In the final article, this component is announced but reported elsewhere.

## SLIDE 20

- Analysis populations:
- in the final article, the main population corresponds to all randomised and eligible participants;
- one participant was withdrawn after randomisation because finally ineligible;
- therefore, in practice, we have a modified ITT, not a strict ITT.
- Efficacy and safety analyses are performed on this same set.
- No detailed per-protocol population is highlighted in the main text.
- The initial protocol announced that primary and secondary efficacy analyses would be performed by intention to treat.
- It also specified that mechanistic and economic analyses would be performed only in subjects with the necessary data, without imputation.
- This is an important nuance:
- not everything is analysed on exactly the same basis depending on the nature of the objectives.

## SLIDE 21

- Statistical hypotheses and sample size calculation:
- clinically relevant difference fixed at 3 points on the QIDS-SR16 at 12 weeks;
- assumed standard deviation of 5.4;
- expected standardised effect size of about 0.56;
- power of 90%;
- alpha of 5% for the primary clinical objective.
- Under this hypothesis, 68 patients per group are necessary without lost to follow-up.
- The initial protocol, with 20% anticipated lost to follow-up, provided for 170 patients in total for the clinical objective alone, and 204 if one also wanted to protect the alpha risk for the mechanistic component.
- The final article reports an adaptation of the calculation:
- the actual observed rate of lost to follow-up was lower, around 8%;
- the estimate was therefore revised to 74 per group, i.e., 148, for the primary endpoint.
- In the final article, the authors add that by also taking into account the mechanistic component with an alpha lowered to 2.5%, 89 patients per group would have been needed, i.e., 178 in total.
- Important oral point:
- there was adaptation of a feasibility parameter, not opportunistic change of the primary endpoint.


## SLIDE 22

- Main statistical analysis:
- generalised linear mixed model for longitudinal data;
- use of repeated measures of QIDS-SR16;
- fixed effects: group, time, group x time interaction, minimisation variables, and baseline level;
- random effects: participant, and in the final article site as random effect.
- The subtle point is the following:
- the primary endpoint is defined at 12 weeks;
- but the article specifies that the model uses all QIDS-SR16 data collected up to 48 weeks.
- The initial protocol described rather an analysis based on weekly measures up to 12 weeks.
- The estimation of the effect at 12 weeks is therefore extracted from a broader longitudinal model.
- It is defensible, because it improves the efficiency of the estimation.
- But it is slightly unusual to comment on at the oral presentation, because data posterior to the most controlled phase participate in the modelling.

## SLIDE 23

- Secondary analyses:
- for continuous variables, adjusted mean differences;
- for binary variables, mixed logistic model then expression of results as relative risks;
- for count data, incidence ratios.
- Adjustments:
- randomised group;
- age;
- sex;
- baseline QIDS-SR16 score;
- time x group interaction;
- sometimes initial value of the concerned endpoint depending on the analysed endpoint;
- site as random effect;
- participant as random intercept for longitudinal data.
- To say to the jury:
- the idea of a fixed effect is a factor that we explicitly adjust;
- the idea of a random effect is acknowledging variability between subjects or between centres without seeking to estimate a coefficient specific to each unit.

## SLIDE 24

- Management of missing data:
- the main text does not describe simple imputation of last observation carried forward type, which is rather reassuring;
- the main analysis relies on a mixed model using available data;
- in practice, this assumes that missing data are at least compatible with a MAR type hypothesis, i.e., missing at random conditionally on the variables of the model;
- for response and remission at 12 weeks, the authors specify that there was no imputation;
- they therefore analyse subjects having a datum available in the planned window.
- Sensitivity analyses are mentioned but referred to the appendix.
- Therefore, critical point:
- the basic strategy is standard and acceptable;
- but we cannot discuss in detail, from the body of the article alone, the robustness to MNAR scenarios.

## SLIDE 25

- Protocol modifications and operational aspects:
- no modification of outcomes after the start of the study;
- modification of the lost to follow-up parameter used for sample size calculation;
- recruitment delayed by the COVID-19 pandemic;
- participants recruited after September 2023 with shorter follow-up;
- but all had time to reach week 12.
- Methodological consequence:
- the robustness of the main result is not called into question;
- on the other hand, analyses at 48 weeks rely on a more restricted subset of participants.

## SLIDE 26

- Participant flow:
- 217 participants seen at screening;
- 66 excluded before analytical inclusion;
- 151 randomised;
- 1 participant withdrawn after randomisation because ineligible;
- 150 participants analysed, 75 per group.
- Data available at the time of the primary endpoint:
- 68 out of 75 in the pramipexole group;
- 67 out of 75 in the placebo group.
- At 48 weeks, only 40 participants on pramipexole and 39 on placebo reached this duration before closure.
- For the oral presentation:
- one must distinguish loss of data at 12 weeks, treatment discontinuation, complete withdrawal from study, and impossibility to reach 48 weeks before closure.

## SLIDE 27

- Baseline characteristics:
- mean age 44.9 years;
- 56% women;
- mean QIDS-SR16 score 16.3, thus severe depression on average;
- median duration of the current episode 8.5 years;
- mean number of ineffective treatments in the current episode 3.5;
- about 21% already on pharmacological augmentation strategy.
- Therefore, the population is clinically heavy:
- chronic disease;
- strong resistance;
- significant functional impact.
- This aspect reinforces the clinical relevance of the observed signal.

## SLIDE 28

- Main result at 12 weeks:
- mean decrease in QIDS-SR16 of 6.4 points under pramipexole;
- decrease of 2.4 points under placebo;
- adjusted mean difference of -3.91 points;
- 95% CI from -5.37 to -2.45;
- p < 0.0001.
- This result is both:
- statistically very significant;
- compatible with clinical relevance, since the threshold retained for sample size calculation was 3 points;
- consistent with a large standardised effect size, around 0.87.
- Possible oral formulation:
- we do not just see a small statistical signal;
- we observe a gap that exceeds the threshold considered a priori as clinically important.

## SLIDE 29

- Secondary results at 12 weeks:
- response 44% versus 16%;
- RR 2.72;
- remission 28% versus 8%;
- RR 3.94;
- improvement also on clinician QIDS;
- improvement on SHAPS, thus anhedonia;
- improvement on GAD-7, thus anxiety.
- Methodological interest:
- the consistency of the signal on several dimensions reduces the hypothesis of an isolated or aberrant result;
- the fact that the clinician QIDS goes in the same direction as the self-assessed QIDS reinforces credibility a bit despite the risk of unblinding.

## SLIDE 30

- Results at 48 weeks:
- maintenance of a benefit on QIDS-SR16 with adjusted difference of -2.02;
- improvement of WSAS;
- superior treatment satisfaction.
- But one must be cautious:
- not all patients had 48 weeks of follow-up;
- antidepressant modifications were allowed after 12 weeks;
- they were more frequent in the placebo group.
- Therefore, the rigorous message is:
- the maintenance signal is interesting;
- but the level of causality is stronger at 12 weeks than at 48 weeks.

## SLIDE 31

- Tolerability:
- almost all participants reported at least one adverse event;
- discontinuation for intolerance 20% under pramipexole versus 5% under placebo;
- RR 3.95.
- Frequent adverse effects:
- nausea;
- headache;
- dizziness;
- drowsiness;
- sleep disorders;
- vomiting.
- Specific signals monitored:
- impulse control disorders;
- manic symptoms;
- psychosis;
- suicidal ideation.
- Two cases of impulse control disorders were observed under pramipexole and regressed after dose reduction.

## SLIDE 32

- Bias and methodological limitations:
- self-assessed primary endpoint;
- significant functional unblinding at the end of the study;
- exclusion after randomisation of one patient, thus modified ITT;
- limited details in the main text on sensitivity analyses;
- significant selection of patients;
- absence of ethnicity data.
- Unblinding deserves to be discussed separately:
- 77% of placebo participants and 71% of pramipexole participants correctly guessed their group;
- p < 0.001.
- As the primary endpoint is self-assessed, this can increase an expectation effect.
- This is probably the main threat to internal validity.

## SLIDE 33

- Methodological strengths:
- randomised, multicentre, placebo-controlled trial;
- centralised allocation;
- prespecified analysis plan;
- run-in to limit baseline inflation;
- strict control of co-interventions up to 12 weeks;
- dense longitudinal follow-up by digital platform and regular calls;
- consistency between several efficacy endpoints;
- truly resistant population, thus relevant clinical question.
- In short:
- good global internal validity;
- credible main result;
- but not totally immune to the bias of imperfect blinding.

## SLIDE 34

- External validity:
- good for adult patients with treatment-resistant unipolar depression followed in specialised psychiatry in the UK;
- more limited for general practice, patients with severe comorbidities, bipolarity, psychosis, or history of impulsivity;
- tolerability may be less favorable outside the trial.
- Clinical scope:
- pramipexole appears as a plausible augmentation option;
- not as a definitive new standard;
- ideally, direct comparative trials versus strategies already used, for example quetiapine or lithium, would be needed, with a focus on tolerability and cost-effectiveness.

## SLIDE 35

- Cautious conclusion:
- the study shows quite convincingly that at 12 weeks, in patients with treatment-resistant unipolar depression already on antidepressant, pramipexole improves symptoms more than a placebo.
- The signal is strong, consistent, and clinically plausible.
- On the other hand, tolerability is poorer.
- The level of confidence is good for short-term efficacy in this selected population.
- It is more moderate for:
- generalisation to broader populations;
- effect at very long term;
- exact magnitude of the effect if blinding is imperfect.
- Possible closing sentence:
- this study supports the use of pramipexole as an augmentation option in certain resistant patients, but with rigorous selection, close monitoring, and without exaggerating the scope of the result.

## Possible jury questions

### Question 1

Why is placebo an acceptable comparator here?

Answer: because there is no pharmacological augmentation treatment unanimously recognised as a first-line standard in this situation. The placebo therefore allows cleanly isolating the additive effect of pramipexole on an antidepressant background.

### Question 2

Why is the run-in phase methodologically interesting?

Answer: it allows verifying adherence to data collection, symptomatic stability, and limiting baseline inflation, i.e., the exaggeration of the initial score. On the other hand, it selects more stable and more adherent subjects.

### Question 3

What is meant by baseline inflation?

Answer: it is the fact that a starting score is artificially high, for example because of the selection context or patient expectations. If not controlled, one can overestimate subsequent improvement.

### Question 4

Is the study really intention-to-treat?

Answer: not strictly. The authors use the term ITT, but a randomised patient was withdrawn after randomisation because ineligible. In practice, it is therefore a modified ITT.

### Question 5

Why is randomisation by minimisation interesting here?

Answer: because with a moderate sample size and several centres, it limits imbalances on important prognostic factors such as age, sex, centre, and initial severity. It is particularly useful when one wants comparable groups without a very large sample size.

### Question 6

Did the double blind really work?

Answer: technically yes at the start, but functionally imperfect afterwards. The adverse effects of pramipexole probably helped participants guess their group. This is a significant bias, especially with a self-assessed primary endpoint.

### Question 7

Why can the choice of QIDS-SR16 as primary endpoint be discussed?

Answer: it is a validated tool, sensitive to change, and easy to collect repeatedly. But as it is self-assessed, it is more vulnerable to an expectation bias than a totally rater-based or more objective endpoint.

### Question 8

How did the authors manage missing data?

Answer: for the main analysis, they use a mixed model on available data, which amounts to a valid approach under MAR hypothesis. For response and remission at 12 weeks, they specify that there was no imputation. Full details of sensitivity analyses are in the appendix.

### Question 9

What is a MAR hypothesis?

Answer: this means that the probability that a datum is missing depends on the information already observed, but not on the true unobserved value once this information is taken into account in the model. It is a frequent hypothesis, but not directly verifiable.

### Question 10

Why is it a bit particular to use all data up to 48 weeks to estimate a main effect at 12 weeks?

Answer: because the primary endpoint is defined at 12 weeks, while the model also borrows information from other measurement times. This can improve precision, but it deserves to be explained because the post-12 weeks phase is less controlled.

### Question 11

Are the secondary analyses solid?

Answer: they are globally adapted and consistent with the main analysis. On the other hand, they remain secondary. They reinforce the consistency of the signal, but should not be interpreted as if each had the same demonstrative strength as the primary endpoint.

### Question 12

Why are the results at 48 weeks less causally convincing?

Answer: because part of the patients could not reach 48 weeks before closure, and because after 12 weeks antidepressants could be modified. The contrast between groups therefore becomes less pure.

### Question 13

What to think of the benefit-risk ratio?

Answer: it seems interesting in very resistant patients, because the symptomatic gain is clear. But there is a non-negligible cost in tolerability, with more treatment discontinuations and dopaminergic effects to monitor.

### Question 14

What is the main strength of internal validity?

Answer: the association of a centralised randomisation, a placebo comparator, a prespecified protocol, strict control of co-interventions up to 12 weeks, and dense longitudinal follow-up.

### Question 15

What is the main limitation of external validity?

Answer: the population is very selected, specialised, and certain high-risk profiles, such as subjects with history of impulsivity, psychosis, or bipolarity, were excluded. Therefore, one cannot transpose without caution.

### Question 16

Why is having a clinician QIDS also useful?

Answer: because it brings a different source of evaluation from the self-questionnaire alone. The fact that both go in the same direction reinforces credibility of the signal a bit, even if it does not totally remove the unblinding problem.

### Question 17

What does this study really show, and what does it not show?

Answer: it shows a short-term clinical benefit of pramipexole as an adjunct to an antidepressant in selected patients with resistant unipolar depression. It does not yet show that it is the best augmentation treatment, nor that this benefit is generalisable to all patients or perfectly durable in the long term.
